It is important to note that many factors can be taken into account in order to declare a reference “weak” (basically unproblematic) instead of “strong” (which requires stability at the risk of breaking the HTML specification). These include:

  • Interfacing through clean imports of named items, in such a way that HTML is sufficiently shielded from the internal details of the import.
  • The reference (or its applicable parts) is deployed in such a way that changes to it that could affect HTML would cause major breakage in non-negligible amounts of deployed content. Such changes are therefore considered impossible.
  • The reference is broadly implemented, tested, and/or deployed; and if the specification defining it is not yet “stable” it is only due to incidental issues (e.g. unavailable editor, overwhelming process). It effectively cannot change in a breaking manner.

The amount of usage and the granularity of the reference also matter. For instance, a good case could be made for the DOM to be a weak reference but it is of such importance that we chose to err on the side of marking it “strong”. Conversely, some massive specification may be imported (e.g. ASN.1) but the reference is fine-grained to a small part and is therefore well-contained, classifying it as within the realm of safety.
